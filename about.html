About

Health information, with its sources showing.

Lean Theory is a one-person project. I'm not a doctor, dietitian, or personal trainer — I'm someone who got tired of health and fitness advice that contradicted itself every other week, and started reading the actual research to figure out what holds up. This site is where I write up what I find.

My approach

I'd rather be useful than viral

Most health content online is optimized for engagement, not accuracy — and the two often pull in opposite directions. A nuanced, "it depends" answer rarely outperforms a confident, oversimplified one. I've made a deliberate choice to publish the nuanced version anyway.

Because I'm not a credentialed expert, I lean hard on sources that are: systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and guidance from major health bodies. Every article points back to the kind of evidence it draws from, and says clearly when the research is mixed, preliminary, or just not there yet. The "Facts Panel" on each piece is my attempt to make that legible at a glance — including an honest "evidence level" — the same way a nutrition label tells you what's actually in the package before you commit to it.

None of this is medical advice, and it isn't a substitute for talking to a qualified professional who knows your situation. Think of Lean Theory as a starting point for asking better questions — not the final word.

What I Optimize For

Three things I won't compromise on

Evidence first

Confidence matches the data

If the research is strong, I'll say so plainly. If it's preliminary or mixed, the article will say that too — even if a more confident headline would perform better.

Plain language

No jargon without translation

Terms like "anabolic," "glycemic," or "inflammatory" get explained the first time they show up — I'd rather a sentence run long than leave you guessing.

Honest about limits

I'll tell you what I don't know

I'm not an expert, and I won't pretend to be one. When something is outside what the evidence can answer — or outside what a non-professional should weigh in on — I'll say so and point you toward someone qualified.

Who writes this

One curious person, writing as "Max"

Max

The pen name behind Lean Theory. Not a medical professional — just someone who reads the research carefully and tries to explain it honestly.

"Max" is a pen name, and I'll be upfront about why: I'd rather you judge what's written here by its sources and reasoning than by a name or a string of letters after it. Everything on Lean Theory is researched and written by one person — me — and published under that name.

I don't have a background in medicine, nutrition, or fitness coaching. What I do have is a habit of chasing claims back to their original studies, a low tolerance for "trust me" health advice, and a commitment to telling you when the evidence is thin. If I get something wrong, I want to hear about it — corrections go straight to the inbox on the contact page, and I update articles when they need it.

For anything specific to your own body, health conditions, or goals, please talk to a qualified professional. Lean Theory is here to help you understand the landscape — not to replace personalized care.
