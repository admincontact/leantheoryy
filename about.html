About

Health information, with its sources showing.

Lean Theory started as a shared document between a personal trainer and a dietitian who were tired of sending the same myth-busting links to friends. It's now a small, independent publication — still doing the same thing, just for more people.

Our approach

We'd rather be useful than viral

Most health content online is optimized for engagement, not accuracy — and the two often pull in opposite directions. A nuanced, "it depends" answer rarely outperforms a confident, oversimplified one. We've made a deliberate choice to publish the nuanced version anyway.

Every article on this site links back to the kind of sources it draws from — systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and guidance from major health bodies — and we say clearly when the evidence is mixed, preliminary, or just not there yet. The "Facts Panel" on each piece is our attempt to make that legible at a glance, the same way a nutrition label tells you what's actually in the package before you commit to it.

What We Optimize For

Three things we won't compromise on

Evidence first

Confidence matches the data

If the research is strong, we'll say so plainly. If it's preliminary or mixed, the article will say that too — even if a more confident headline would perform better.

Plain language

No jargon without translation

Terms like "anabolic," "glycemic," or "inflammatory" get explained the first time they show up — we'd rather a sentence run long than leave you guessing.

Sustainable defaults

Built for the long run

Our recipes, routines, and programs are designed to fit into an ordinary week — not a temporary sprint you'll abandon by February.
