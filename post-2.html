What's the secret behind Salman Khan and John Abraham's fitness? We looked past the headlines.

JM By Jordan Mensah · June 2026 · 9 min read

Type almost any Bollywood actor's name into a search bar followed by the word "diet" and you'll get a wall of nearly identical articles: a six-day training split, "clean eating," six small meals, and a quote about discipline. Salman Khan and John Abraham are two of the most searched examples — both known for physiques that have held up for decades, and both the subject of an enormous amount of recycled "secret routine" content.

Rather than add another version of that same article, we went through what's publicly been reported by trainers, interviews, and profiles over the years, and asked a more useful question: which parts of this are genuinely instructive, and which parts only make sense if you're a working actor with a trainer, a chef, and a film role riding on the result?

What's reported about Salman Khan's routine

Salman Khan's training has been described in interviews and trainer profiles as built around a fairly traditional split: alternating weight-training days (chest, back, shoulders, arms) with cardio and abs-focused days, typically across five to six days a week. He's been known for high-rep bodyweight work — large numbers of push-ups and crunches worked into warm-ups and finishers — alongside more conventional lifts like deadlifts and bench press.

More recently, reporting around his role in Sikandar describes a six-day training split designed by his long-time trainer, combined with a calorie-monitored diet built around lean protein, complex carbs, and limited fat, plus martial arts and functional training to support flexibility and athleticism alongside the strength work. Some profiles also mention a period of time-restricted eating (commonly described as a 16:8 schedule), paired with cycling as a preferred form of cardio.

As for the philosophy behind it, Salman summed it up himself at a fitness expo: "the body is made out of the gym" — his framing was that the one or two hours spent training matter less than the discipline carried through the rest of the day.

What's reported about John Abraham's routine

John Abraham's reported approach leans heavily on the idea that nutrition does more work than training. Profiles consistently describe him crediting diet as the larger share of the result — with training contributing the rest — and emphasizing consistency over years rather than short bursts of effort.

His training is generally described as a six-day split pairing a larger muscle group with a smaller one each day (chest and triceps, back and abs, shoulders and biceps, legs), with cardio — often cycling — worked in on separate days. Because Abraham has spoken about following a largely vegetarian diet, his protein sources are reported to lean on paneer, soy, lentils, egg whites, and fish, spread across five or six smaller meals, alongside whey protein as a supplement.

One detail that gets repeated a lot: for a particularly lean, vascular look in the film Force 2, he's reported to have eaten a very large number of egg whites per day during that specific preparation period — a figure he's since said he scaled back significantly. That's worth sitting with for a second, because it's a good example of the gap between "what got someone camera-ready for six weeks" and "what someone actually does most of the time."

The most repeated "secret" in both routines isn't a food or an exercise. It's that the routine has existed, in some form, for twenty-plus years.

The parts that are genuinely useful

Strip away the specifics, and a few patterns show up in both stories — and in the broader research on body composition — often enough to take seriously:

  • A repeatable structure. Both routines are described as splits — a plan for which muscle groups get trained on which days — rather than improvised sessions. Having a structure removes a lot of the daily decision-making that causes workouts to get skipped.
  • Protein spread across several meals. Five or six smaller meals each containing a protein source is a pattern that shows up across both, and lines up with what we covered in our piece on protein timing — consistency across the day matters more than any single meal.
  • Cardio that doesn't feel like punishment. Cycling, in particular, comes up for both — something closer to a hobby than a chore, which tends to be far more sustainable than forcing yourself onto a treadmill you dread.
  • Treating it as identity, not a phase. Both are described as having maintained some version of this for two decades or more. The specific program matters less than the fact that a version of it kept happening.

The parts that don't transfer — and one that's worth a warning

It's worth being honest about what's different here. Reported training volumes in the three-hour-a-day range, year-round access to a personal trainer and dietitian, and meals prepared specifically around a filming schedule are resources most people simply don't have — and arguably don't need, since the research on diminishing returns suggests far less training is required for most health and physique goals.

The egg-white example deserves a specific caution. Very high intakes of any single food, sustained for weeks, are typically a short-term strategy used under professional supervision for a specific outcome (in this case, an on-screen look) — not a template for daily eating. Extremely high protein intakes from a narrow set of foods can create practical issues (digestive discomfort, nutrient gaps from foods you're no longer eating) and aren't something to copy without guidance from a registered dietitian.

The actual takeaway

If there's a "secret," it's an unsatisfying one: a training split you'll actually follow, protein at most meals, a form of cardio you don't hate, and enough years for all of that to compound. None of that requires a film budget — which might be exactly why it doesn't make for as good a headline.
